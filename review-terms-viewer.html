Lost In Tech · Review Framework

How reviews work here.
Plain English.

Every hardware review on this channel operates under a published, public framework. Here's what that actually means for you as a viewer — no legal jargon.

Why does this exist? When a company sends hardware for review, there's often pressure — subtle or not — to produce favourable content. This framework exists to make it structurally impossible for that to happen. If a company won't agree to these terms, the hardware doesn't arrive. Simple as that.
01 · Editorial Independence
1.1
No previews. No drafts. No approvals. Companies never see the video before you do.
✗ Draft review ✗ Pre-publication approval ✗ Script review ✓ Published direct to you

The first time anyone at the manufacturer sees the finished video is when it goes public — same as you. There is no back-channel review process, no "technical accuracy check" that doubles as editorial control, and no opportunity to request changes before release.

Why it matters: Pre-publication approval is how positive-only reviews happen. Remove it and the company has no mechanism to soften, edit, or suppress findings they don't like.
1.2
Negatives are fair game. Nothing gets softened because a company asks nicely.
✗ "Please don't mention that" ✗ "Avoid exaggerating shortcomings" ✓ Full honest assessment

If something doesn't work well, that goes in the video. If software is buggy, if build quality is disappointing, if the product doesn't do what it claims — that's exactly the kind of information you need to make a purchasing decision, and it will be in the review.

Why it matters: "Please don't exaggerate shortcomings" is real contract language companies use. It sounds reasonable until you realise it means "don't tell viewers about problems."
1.3
Competitors can always be mentioned. No blackout windows. No restrictions on what else gets covered.
✗ "Don't mention competitors" ✗ "7-day blackout on other brands" ✓ Full comparative freedom

If a competing product does something better, that will be said. If another company's scanner, printer, or tool is more relevant to what you're trying to do — that gets mentioned. No brand agreement restricts what else appears on this channel.

Why it matters: Competitor blackouts are designed to prevent you from hearing "actually, this other product is better for your use case." That's information you deserve.
1.4
Reputation protection clauses are rejected. Test results are test results, regardless of how they reflect on a brand.
✗ "Don't harm our reputation" ✗ "Remove content if we ask" ✓ Results published as found

Some companies include clauses requiring content to be removed if it damages their brand image. Those clauses are refused outright. Engineering results, test data, and honest findings will be published regardless of whether they're flattering.

Why it matters: A company that needs contractual protection from honest reviews is implicitly telling you their product can't survive honest reviews.
02 · Your Content, Your Viewing
2.1
Videos stay on this channel. No re-uploaded, re-edited versions appearing on YouTube elsewhere.
✗ Re-uploaded to brand YouTube ✗ Edited brand versions on YouTube ✓ Original stays here ✓ Embed on brand website OK

Manufacturers cannot re-upload or re-edit the video to their own YouTube channel. This protects you from ending up watching what appears to be an independent review but is actually a brand-controlled edit. The original video, in its original form, lives here.

Why it matters: A re-edited version of a review on a brand's channel could remove negative sections, change context, or present the content as something it isn't.
2.2
No paid advertising without a separate agreement. Sending hardware doesn't buy advertising rights.
✗ Google Ads using review footage ✗ Paid social using review clips ✓ Organic social sharing OK ✓ Paid ads require separate contract

A product loan in exchange for a review does not automatically grant the right to use that review footage in paid advertising campaigns. If a company wants to run ads using this content, that requires a separate commercial agreement — which is a different thing entirely from an independent review.

Why it matters: Without this clause, a company could send a £500 scanner and end up with thousands of pounds worth of ad creative featuring an "independent" review they didn't pay for.
2.3
Content meaning cannot be altered. Including by AI voice synthesis or selective clipping.
✗ AI-altered speech ✗ Out-of-context clipping ✓ Short clips with proper credit OK ✓ Context must be preserved

Short clips can be shared on social platforms with proper credit — but the meaning and context of what was said cannot be changed. This explicitly covers AI voice synthesis, which could technically make it sound like something different was said, and selective clipping that removes important context.

Why it matters: A clip of "this scanner is impressive" pulled from a sentence that continued "...but not at this price point" is misinformation. This clause prevents that.
03 · Timelines & Process
3.1
Reviews take as long as they take. Corporate promotional schedules don't override thorough testing.
✗ Rushed to meet launch windows ✗ Guaranteed delivery dates ✓ Typically ~1 month from delivery ✓ Thoroughness over speed

Reviews typically take around a month from receiving hardware. That's not laziness — it's bench testing, real-world use, scripting, filming, and editing done properly. No corporate promotional deadline will rush that process, because a rushed review serves you less well.

Why it matters: Launch-day reviews produced under time pressure are often surface-level. Problems that only emerge with extended use don't get found. A month of testing finds things a weekend review won't.
3.2
Problems during testing get documented. Issues, fixes, and workarounds all go in the video.
✗ "Please don't show that bug" ✓ Issues documented transparently ✓ Technical support consulted ✓ Resolution process shown

If something goes wrong during testing, the manufacturer's technical team will be consulted. But the original problem, the troubleshooting process, and the outcome — whether resolved or not — are all fair game for inclusion in the final video. Real-world problems are useful information.

Why it matters: You're going to encounter the same problems. Knowing they exist, and knowing how to fix them, is more valuable than a review that pretended everything was perfect.
04 · The Hardware Itself
4.1
Hardware is kept unless collected. It's payment for testing time, not a loan.
✓ Kept after review published ✓ Can appear in future content ✗ No leverage over content

Unless the manufacturer arranges and pays for return shipping before delivery, hardware sent for review is kept. This matters because hardware that has to be returned can be used as leverage — "send us a positive review or we'll ask for it back." Keeping it removes that leverage entirely.

Why it matters: A reviewer who might have to return hardware has a subtle incentive to keep the manufacturer happy. Remove that incentive and the review is more honest.
4.2
Testing isn't always gentle. Real-world evaluation sometimes leaves marks.
✓ All care taken ✗ Compensation for testing wear

Thorough hardware evaluation sometimes involves processes that leave equipment in a different condition than it arrived. Every effort is made to handle products carefully, but normal testing wear isn't something that can be compensated for. This is disclosed upfront so there are no surprises.

Why it matters: A reviewer worried about damaging hardware might avoid tests that would reveal important information. This clause removes that concern.
The one-line summary
What this means for you: When you watch a review on this channel, no part of what you're seeing was approved, softened, timed, or influenced by the manufacturer. The product was tested independently, the results were documented honestly, and the video was published without anyone outside this channel seeing it first. That's the only way a review can actually be useful to you.
